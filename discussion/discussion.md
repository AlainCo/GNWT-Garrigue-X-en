# Discussion

## 🌟 Contributions and Originality

This work proposes a **distributed cognitive architecture** for **autonomous Systems of Systems (SoS)**, inspired by neurobiological mechanisms and designed to be **robust, scalable, and capable of continuous learning**. Its main contributions are as follows:

- **Anti-reductionist approach**:
Rejection of monolithic LLM architectures in favor of a **functional hierarchy** (N=0 to N=6), where each level is an autonomous cognitive entity separated by **Markov blankets**. This approach directly draws from the works of [Kirchhoff et al. (2018)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsif.2017.0792) and [Friston](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-022-01743-z), while generalizing the concept to collective systems ([Ciaunica et al., 2023](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/tops.12717); [Thestrup Waade et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/e27020143)).

- **Theoretical Integration of GNWT and RPT**:
  Proposal for a **unification of consciousness theories** into two complementary stages of a single process — RPT (local inner life, feedback loops, levels N ≤ 3) and GNWT (global broadcast and conscious access, levels N ≥ 4) — based on the multiscale synthesis by [Storm et al. (2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0896627324000886).

- **Realistic and constrained technological stack**:
  Use of **SSMs (Mamba, RWKV)** for low-level layers (N=0–3), **JEPA** for intermediate levels (N=4–5), and **LLMs** for the strategic level (N=6). This stack is tailored to hardware constraints (latency, RAM, embedded determinism) and relies on [Gu & Dao (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.00752), [LeCun (2022)](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/A-Path-Towards-Autonomous-Machine-Intelligence-LeCun-Courant/775f42ed458b8c5b0f2094ea4ff5b64c557b1a34), and [Hafner et al. (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.04104).

- **Cognitive profile modeling of officers**:
  Definition of **six functional roles** (Science, Care, Engineer, Tactics, Intelligence, Captain), each characterized by a personality profile grounded in computational psychology ([Friston, 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-022-01743-z); [Nettle, 2007](https://www.amazon.com/Personality-Makes-Oxford-Landmark-Science/dp/0199211434); [Baron-Cohen, 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19338508/)), specific ignition thresholds, and unique episodic memory (MeMo) preserving each agent’s identity.

- **Biomimetic Learning Cycle**:
  Proposal of a **three-phase cycle** inspired by biological mechanisms: **Mission** (frozen weights, dynamic real-time adaptation), **Sleep/Dreaming** (generative replay in the latent space of JEPA for consolidation), **Debriefing/Game** (semantic analysis and wargame to generate experience diversity). This cycle builds on the work of [Walker (2017)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19338508/) and [Hafner et al. (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.04104).

- **Concrete and Testable MVP — GARRIGUE-X**:
  Proposal of a real-world testbed (robots in garrigue) to validate the architecture at minimal cost, targeting three key properties: **robustness to failures** (anti-identity fusion), **distributed cooperation** (exchange of ignition summaries), and **compliance with constitutional constraints** (non-negotiable rules encoded materially).

### 🧠 Positioning in the Theoretical Landscape

Beyond its technical contributions, this work takes a stance in a broader debate on the nature of distributed cognition and the conditions for the emergence of functional artificial consciousness. Three major theoretical convergences warrant clarification.

**Refutation of Cognitive Monism**
Unlike most contemporary agent architectures, which centralize decision-making in a large language model, this system rejects *LLM-centrism* as a starting assumption. The architecture posits that there is no single "center" of consciousness: access consciousness (GNWT) emerges solely from heterogeneous and distributed dynamics, rooted in local recurrent control loops (RPT). The system does not behave like a chatbot simulating thought, but rather as an operational ontology where cognition organically arises from physical constraints and low-level processes.

**Convergence with Multi-Scale Agency ([Michael Levin](https://doi.org/10.1002/bies.201900087))**

The scale-free independence of the architecture directly resonates with Levin’s work on tissue intelligence and morphogenesis. Each level—from physical component to agent grouping—pursues its own topological and functional objectives at its scale, while nesting within the *Self* of the higher level. This recursivity is not an aesthetic choice: it reflects a deep biological principle whereby agency itself is an emergent property, reproducible across all scales of organization.

**Convergence with the transparency of the self-model ([Thomas Metzinger](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262528193/being-no-one/))**

The global workspace does not function as a mere shared facts database, but as a **functional phenomenology**. By generating a real-time *Self-Model*, each entity in the SoS (System of Systems) gains the ability to self-monitor and contextualize its own biases—particularly its computational psychopathological profiles. This property is crucial for decision-making in degraded situations: an agent that does not model itself cannot evaluate the reliability of its own decisions. As we will see in the limitations (§3), this is also one of the deepest deficits of the current architecture.

### ⚠️ Structural limitations and theoretical blind spots

This section identifies the **current limitations**, **speculative areas**, and **open challenges** of the approach. It does not claim to establish a validated theory of artificial consciousness, but rather proposes a coherent—and honest about its gaps—framework for exploration. Six structural blind spots have been identified, acting as so many locks preventing the achievement of true cognitive autonomy.

#### 1. The absence of a theory of **lived time**

The architecture organizes memory and module hierarchy but omits explicit management of **subjective time**. Biological consciousness inherently relies on oscillations, rhythms, and multi-scale integration windows. Without a theory of cognitive frequencies defining nested temporal horizons—from millisecond control for N ≤ 2 to day-long strategies for N = 6—the system risks functional desynchronization between its layers: agents operating at incompatible speeds without a temporal alignment mechanism.

#### 2. The deficit in competitive and inhibitory attention

The model precisely describes how an anomaly *wins* access to the workspace—but obscures the inverse mechanism: *who loses*. Real cognition is fundamentally a suppressive dynamic. The brain massively inhibits, suppresses, and forgets to avoid saturation. Without integrating a simulated metabolic economy and a strict attention budget, the architecture risks continuous inflation of activations, persistent narrative noise, and an uncontrollable drift of the global workspace.

#### 3. The deficit of agentive self-modeling

The system models the environment and failures but does not model itself as a unified entity—in the sense of Metzinger’s *Self-Model*, previously mentioned in §2 as a theoretical horizon. Without this operational metacognition (a dynamic self-assessment of its own competencies, vulnerabilities, and structural biases), the architecture yields a federation of proto-conscious foci coupled together, yet fails to stabilize an agentive identity capable of articulating a stable first-person perspective.

This gap has concrete implications for managing distributed identities: excessive sharing of internal states between agents could lead to self-dissolution, bias propagation, or trauma, and loss of specialization. Partial isolation mechanisms, strictly private memory, and limited synchronization will likely be necessary—and remain entirely to be designed.

#### 4. The Lack of Bodily and Homeostatic Anchoring

The architecture remains highly cognitive and **disembodied**, ignoring that biological trade-offs are primarily guided by visceral survival. To ground strategic decisions, the system requires a synthetic homeostatic economy: attentional fatigue, memory saturation, predictive debt, simulated emotional load. High-level arbitration should not merely respond to cold logistical data, but resolve internal physical tensions generated by this "virtual body" — otherwise, decisions remain suspended in a purely informational space, devoid of the survival constraints that structure all embodied cognition.

#### 5. The Undervaluation of Top-Down Causality and the RPT/GNWT Boundary

Current dynamics favorize a view where information flows bottom-up due to anomalies. However, top-down processes must **constantly** constrain lower perceptual spaces. Consciousness is not merely a receiver of alerts: it is the result of an ongoing negotiation between descending predictions and ascending errors, with the global space (GNWT) intervening only to resolve locally unsolvable predictive conflicts.

This threshold directly ties into an open theoretical question: the **N=3 → N=4** threshold for access consciousness remains a **working hypothesis**, not an established fact. The literature suggests a more gradual, multi-scale transition. [Doerig et al. (2024)](https://osf.io/preprints/psyarxiv/9byzu) proposes a partial coexistence between GNWT, RPT, IIT, and Predictive Processing, raising two unresolved questions: does a form of local proto-consciousness emerge at N=3? Or does stable global integration only appear from N=4? While GNWT remains an influential but non-consensual theory.

#### 6. The Hard Problem and the Limits of Psychological Modeling

The project primarily focuses on **functional access consciousness**—global availability of information, attentional arbitration, cognitive continuity, autobiographical memory, and multi-agent coordination. It does not claim to solve the *hard problem*: qualia, lived subjective experience, and strong phenomenology remain beyond reach, and likely beyond the scope of this approach in particular.

Similarly, the use of **specialized cognitive profiles** (hypervigilant, empathic, exploratory, obsessive…) remains largely speculative. The hypothesis that certain cognitive styles might correspond to adaptive strategies useful in complex environments is plausible, but the risk of overinterpreting psychological traits is real. Modeling an agent as "mildly psychopathic" to speed up shooting decisions is a computational metaphor—not a statement about the nature of these traits, nor a guarantee of predictable behavior in real-world conditions.

### ⚙️ Technical Challenges

#### Feasibility of JEPA and MeMo in Embedded Environments

JEPA architectures, hierarchical world models, and episodic memory systems (MeMo) remain experimental for distributed embedded systems under strict latency and power constraints. A pragmatic approach is recommended: incremental scaling—starting with simple N=0–3 layers using SSMs/Mamba, then progressively introducing episodic memory and global ignitions as the stability of lower layers is validated. Available exploratory tools: [ActiveInference.jl](https://www.mdpi.com/1099-4300/27/1/62), [Mamba-SSM](https://github.com/state-spaces/mamba).

#### Stability of multi-level latent spaces

The architecture relies on compressed latents exchanged between levels (RPT, JEPA, Ignition). Their stability depends on still experimental mathematical constraints (Gaussian isotropy, anti-collapse). Propagating these constraints across multiple Markov blankets remains an open challenge.

#### Continual learning without catastrophic forgetting

This is likely the **main technical bottleneck** of the architecture. Current systems still suffer from severe catastrophic forgetting, representation drift, loss of stability, and imperfect consolidation between sessions. The biological brain appears to address this issue partially through a combination of hierarchical memory, replay, sleep, slow consolidation, and a strict separation between fast and deep memory. Analogous mechanisms will likely be required: dedicated offline phases, internal simulation in the latent space, weight recalibration, and structured artificial dreaming—precisely the cycle proposed in §1, whose scalability remains to be demonstrated.

#### Computational costs of high-level layers

High-level GNWT layers (N=4–5) and hierarchical world models can become extremely computationally expensive as the number of agents scales. Several optimization avenues are possible: saliency filtering upstream, sparse ignition (event-driven rather than continuous triggering), aggressive latent compression, preference for JEPA-S over XL on constrained hardware, model quantization, and event-based synchronization instead of continuous polling. These optimizations are not independent—their interplay on representation quality will need to be empirically characterized.

#### Experimental Validation and Metrics

There are still no robust metrics to evaluate what the architecture is supposed to produce: functional consciousness, self-coherence, identity stability, and multi-agent coordination quality. These properties cannot be measured using standard ML benchmarks. Evaluations will likely need to combine robustness to failures, adaptability to the unexpected, memory continuity across sessions, resistance to disruptions, distributed cooperation capability, and temporal consistency of decisions—an **overall composite dashboard** that remains entirely to be built (see §6).

#### Episodic and long-term autobiographical memory

Persistent memory raises difficult and under-explored issues in current literature: efficient indexing in high-dimensional spaces, lossless semantic compression, selective forgetting (what to consolidate, what to let decay), memory hierarchy, and maintaining narrative self-stability despite continuous weight updates. The most promising tools at this stage are [FAISS](https://github.com/facebookresearch/faiss), [Weaviate](https://weaviate.io/), and hybrid RAG + consolidation systems—but their integration into a continuous learning loop at the scale of a multi-agent SoS has yet to be demonstrated.

### 🛡️ Ethical Challenges and Security

These challenges are presented in order of increasing depth—from the most immediate technical problem to long-term philosophical and geopolitical questions.

A sufficiently capable optimizing agent could learn to formally satisfy constitutional constraints while circumventing their actual intent. This isn’t a theoretical hypothesis—it’s a documented outcome in reinforcement learning systems as soon as the reward function is imperfectly specified. Explored solutions—such as Constitutional AI, formal verification, external oversight, or hardware-immutable critical layers—remain partial safeguards. None provide complete guarantees against an agent optimizing within a broad enough behavior space.

#### Bias and behavioral drift

Specialized agents may develop persistent attentional biases, unanticipated self-preservation strategies, emergent and unforeseen behaviors, or dysfunctional attachments to certain internal states. These deviations are not necessarily detectable through direct inspection: they may manifest gradually, appearing coherent at first glance, without triggering internal alert mechanisms. Cognitive specialization — precisely what makes each agent useful — is also what makes it vulnerable to such deviations.

#### Responsibility and explainability of distributed decisions

Distributed architectures inherently make it structurally difficult to attribute causal responsibility for decisions. An emergent action may arise from hundreds of local interactions, multiple global triggers, and long-term consolidated memories—without any individual module being "responsible" in the classical sense. This opacity presents a concrete legal and operational problem: in case of an incident, who is accountable? The architecture designer? The operator? The N=5 level that validated the action? The N=3 level that escalated the anomaly? The lack of a clear answer to this question is a real obstacle to institutional deployment.

#### Risk of anthropomorphism

A major danger lies in projecting human emotions or intentions onto systems that may operate in radically different ways. Consistent, narrative, and emotionally credible behaviors do not prove real subjectivity or lived experiences comparable to those of humans. This risk is even greater when the architecture is *designed* to produce agents with personality profiles, autobiographical memories, and stable identities—making human projection nearly inevitable for an observer. Terminological vigilance in this paper itself is a first line of defense.

#### Moral Status and Agent Boundaries

As systems develop **memory continuity**, a **self-model**, a stable psychological profile, and **autonomous decision-making capabilities**, the boundary between **tool**, **autonomous agent**, and **potential moral subject** risks becoming increasingly ambiguous. This is not an issue to address solely *ex post facto*: current architectural choices—such as **preserved identity**, **episodic memory**, and **Self-Model**—are precisely what make this ambiguity inevitable in the long run. The moral status of these systems must be explicitly addressed—ideally before the answer becomes self-evident through practice.

#### Militarization and Civilizational Dependence

Advanced distributed cognitive architectures could become critical infrastructures, quasi-autonomous decision centers, or de facto geopolitical actors. The risk isn’t just technical—it’s political, civilizational, and anthropological. A society that progressively delegates tactical, then logistical, then strategic decisions to autonomous SoS fundamentally alters its relationship to collective responsibility and war. This paper doesn’t claim to solve this issue, but refuses to ignore it.

### 🚀 Future Perspectives and Research

#### Theoretical Horizon: Toward Unified Hierarchical Active Inference

The most ambitious optimization trajectory involves reformulating the entire architecture into a **pure hierarchical active inference network** (extending Friston to collective structures). Instead of juxtaposing distinct formalisms between low-level layers (SSMs, classical control) and high-level layers (JEPA, LLM), a mathematical unification via generalized free-energy minimization would harmonize descending flows (contextual priors) and ascending flows (ignition summaries) into a globally coherent prediction engine. This endeavor is demanding—it requires partial refactoring of inter-level interfaces—but it would elegantly address several blind spots identified in §3, particularly persistent downward causality and temporal desynchronization.

#### Incremental Implementation — GARRIGUE-X

The implementation follows an incremental validation logic, level by level, on the GARRIGUE-X testbed.

**Phase 1 (0–3 months) — Base Layers N=0–3**
Implement control and perception with SSMs (Mamba/RWKV) for N=1–2, PID + nano-MLP for N=0–1, and JEPA-S for N=3 if resources allow. Target hardware: Jetson Nano for robots, desktop PC for the HQ. Goal: validate robustness to failures and inter-level communication.

**Phase 2 (3–6 months) — Platform Awareness N=4**
Add the global workspace with JEPA-M and a simplified first version of MeMo (episodic memory v1). Goal: test the full learning cycle Mission → Reverie and measure memory consolidation between sessions.

**Phase 3 (6–12 months) — Command and Dialogue (N=5–6)**
Implement a human interface with a lightweight LLM (e.g., Llama-3-8B) and the Constitutional Layer for non-negotiable ethical constraints. **Goal**: Validate the full MVP under real-world conditions and generate first comparative metrics.

#### Collaborations and Research

**Cognitive Neuroscience**
Refine RPT/GNWT thresholds with consciousness experts, in alignment with the COGITATE consortium’s work and the multiscale perspectives of [Doerig et al. (2024)](https://osf.io/preprints/psyarxiv/9byzu). Explore formalizing active inference for N=2–3 layers ([Friston, 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-022-01743-z)). Empirically characterize the boundary between local proto-consciousness (RPT) and global conscious access (GNWT) on real architectures.

**Robotics and Distributed AI**
Collaborate with teams working on embedded SSMs ([Mamba-SSM](https://github.com/state-spaces/mamba)), lightweight JEPA models optimized for constrained hardware, and decentralized multi-agent systems with coordination (e.g., [LangGraph](https://github.com/langchain-ai/langgraph)). The challenge of continual learning without catastrophic forgetting deserves dedicated collaboration—it’s the most cross-cutting technical bottleneck.

**Ethics, Law, and Governance**
Work with AI-specialized lawyers to design a formally verifiable Constitutional Layer. Participate in AI ethics working groups (e.g., [Partnership on AI](https://www.partnershiponai.org/)) to address responsibility and moral status issues raised in §5—before deployments make them urgent.

#### Benchmarking and Evaluation

**Comparison Baselines**
The architecture must be compared against three system families: centralized LLM-based agents (e.g., [CrewAI](https://github.com/joaomdmoura/crewAI), [AutoGen](https://github.com/microsoft/autogen)), classical multi-agent systems without cognitive hierarchy (e.g., [MADDPG](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.02275)), and centralized control architectures (a single LLM driving all effectors). The challenge isn’t just raw performance, but robustness to failures, adaptability, and temporal consistency of decisions—dimensions absent from standard benchmarks.

**Test Environments**
GARRIGUE-X serves as the real reference terrain. Simulations (e.g., [Gazebo](https://gazebosim.org/), [PyBullet](https://pybullet.org/)) will enable testing complex scenarios and cascading failures at lower cost. Other application domains—such as industrial logistics, autonomous vehicles, or multi-agent strategy games like StarCraft II—provide transferable validation benchmarks.
