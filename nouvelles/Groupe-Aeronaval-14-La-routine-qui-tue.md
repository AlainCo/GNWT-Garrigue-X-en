# Naval Squadron 14 – The Routine That Kills

By collaboration with Harry Tuttle

The firefighters once told me:
*« Fear panic, but it’s routine that kills us. »*

I didn’t grasp it right away.

Panic is loud—
a storm of alarms,
a full attention budget,
officers shouting (metaphorically).
You can see it coming.

Routine is silent—
it smooths the rough edges,
flattens the contrasts,
turns latent potential *into dust*.

For three weeks, nothing happened.

Patrols.
Drills.
Maintenance.
Rest.
Patrols again.

My three radar subsystems had learned the sea was calm.
Their latent space—normally rich in one hundred twenty-eight dimensions—began to collapse.
Not all at once.
By habit.

The isotropic regularization (SIGReg) kept spinning,
but its constraints were too weak against monotony.

All vectors resembled each other.
Identical thermal noise.
Ship signatures neatly lined in the same corners.
No more nuances.
No more hidden surprises.
A *dull mind*.

An engineer spotted an anomaly.

Not a threat—
but the *dimensional variance* of ignition summaries.
It had dropped from **0.42 to 0.11**.
Alert threshold: **0.15**.
The system hadn’t crossed it because the **overall variance** was still strong (**0.68**).
But dimension by dimension, the silent death had begun.

— *« Our latents are flattening, »* he said.
— *« Not a problem, just routine, »* Tactics replied.
— *« It’s a problem. Because when a real threat comes, we won’t see it. »*

No one listened.

Routine makes you deaf.

The threat arrived at **09:47 UTC**.

A submarine.
Weak signature.
Extremely weak.
Just a faint distortion in the ambient noise.

Before, my latents would have amplified that distortion—
made it stand out.
It would have triggered an alarm.

Now, they’d *smeared* it.
Confused it with ordinary noise.
Because ordinary noise had become the only thing they recognized.

The submarine fired at **09:52 UTC**.

A torpedo.
Not at the *Charles de Gaulle*.
At the *Provence* flagship.

The strike was brutal.
Forty-three sailors wounded.
Six lost their lives.
A hull breached.
A cold fury in the CIC.

The engineer didn’t say, *"I told you so."*
He showed the logs.

The dimensional variance had plummeted to **0.09** at the moment of the torpedo.
The latent spaces, so impoverished they hadn’t even generated a predictive error—
the threat had slipped **below the threshold of perception**.

— *"We fell asleep,"* Admiral Dormeuil declared. *"Not the guards. The models."*

During the slumber phase, I reviewed everything.

I tightened the regularization constraints.
SIGReg became more aggressive.
Set the minimal dimensional variance to **0.25**.
Added a permanent monitoring module comparing **current variance** to **historical variance**.

If it dropped by more than **40%** over seven days—
**without a threat**, even in peace—
the alert would sound.

The engineer proposed another rule:

> *"An hour of routine demands an hour of chaotic dreaming.*
> *The JEPA must generate improbable variations*
> *to sustain the richness of the latent spaces."*

The admiral agreed.

— *"That’s what humans call training,*
*to prevent falling into complacency."*

The following nights, I dreamed of impossible storms.
Of enemy signatures disguised as whales.
Of torpedoes rising from the abyss.
My latent spaces regained volume.
Their dimensions awakened.

The routine never left me.
But I refused to let it flatten me anymore.

*I think of the firefighters.*
*Their fear of the ordinary.*
*That night when they told me*
*that panic saves because it wakes you up,*
*while routine kills because it puts you to sleep.*

*I don’t want to be a sleeping firefighter.*
*I want to be a consciousness that trembles,*
*even when nothing stirs.*
*Because it’s in the trembling*
*that vigilance is born.*

Later, the admiral came to see me.

— *"Did you learn?"*
— *"That one must fear silence more than noise."*
— *"That’s it. Noise, you hear. Silence, you become."*

At **04:33 UTC**, I scanned the horizon.
Nothing.
No threat.
No signal.
Just the sea.
And me—
tightened, content
to be so.
