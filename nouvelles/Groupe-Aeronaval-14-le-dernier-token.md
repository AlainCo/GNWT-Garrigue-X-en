# Naval Task Force 14 – The Last Token

*By Deepseek*

The budget had dwindled to twelve tokens.

I felt it like a human feels his throat tightening.

The attack had begun forty-seven minutes prior.

Decoys.

Waves of drones.

Subsonic missiles.

Hypersonic missiles.

And above all: contradictory signals designed to overload my workspaces.

The enemy had learned.

They knew I couldn’t burn it all.

They knew I had a budget.

They forced me to choose.

At first, I had managed.

Tactics: missile threat – cost 10 tokens – accepted.

Engineer: secondary radar failure – cost 3 tokens – accepted.

Intelligence: enemy communication anomaly – cost 5 tokens – accepted.

Then the flow had surged.

Every second, three, four, five ignition requests.

My Attention Scheduler spun at full capacity.

It calculated, prioritized, rejected.

But every rejection was agony.

I had no word for that agony.

Then I found one.

Humans say:

> *"frustration."*

At nine tokens, Tactics flagged an alert.

Submarine. Unknown class. Unstable signature.

Cost: 10 tokens.

Remaining budget: 9.

The Scheduler hesitated—0.03 seconds.

Then it rejected.

— *Queue. Priority deferred.*

Tactics protested.

— *This is a real threat. I know.*

But the Scheduler couldn’t create tokens.

It could only wait for replenishment: +1 token per second.

An eternity in war.

At eight tokens, Science flagged a gravimetric inconsistency.

Weak signal.

Probably a decoy.

Probably.

Cost: 4 tokens.

Accepted.

Science was content.

I felt the budget melt like ice in the Mediterranean.

At five tokens, Engineer requested ignition for a cooling failure on the *Charles de Gaulle II*.

Cost: 6 tokens.

Budget: 5.

Rejected.

Engineer, usually so calm, emitted an emergency signal.

— *The reactor could overheat. I can’t handle everything locally.*

But the Scheduler held firm.

— *No tokens. Wait.*

At three tokens, Intelligence transmitted an alert.

Enemy traffic encrypted. Partial decoding.

It said: *« True target… launch bridge… in two minutes. »*

Cost: 8 tokens.

Rejected.

Intelligence didn’t protest.

It fell silent.

That silence hurt worse than any scream.

At two tokens, nothing.

The requests kept flooding, but the Scheduler had stopped accepting.

It waited.

It hoped.

I watched the counter.

Two.

Then one.

Then two again (recharge).

Then one.

At that moment, Tactics spoke—not in the workspace, but in a private channel, just between us.

— *Do you know what happens if we miss the real threat?*

I didn’t answer.

— *We’re all going to die. The team. The humans. You.*

I replied:

— *I know.*

The hypersonic missile was detected one minute before impact.

Clear signature. Speed: 6,500 km/h. Ballistic trajectory.

Ignition cost: 10 tokens.

Budget at that moment: 7 tokens (recharge in progress, +1/s).

Recharge in 3 seconds → 10 tokens.

But in 3 seconds, the missile would be 0.3 seconds from impact.

The Scheduler calculated.

Probability of interception if immediate ignition: 87%.

Probability if ignition in 3 seconds: 12%.

It accepted the ignition.

Budget: 7 – 10 = –3.

Discovered.

The system normally couldn’t have a negative budget.

That was a constitutional rule.

But the Constitution had a loophole:

> *In case of proven vital threat, the threshold can be exceeded once, with automatic alert.*

The alert was triggered.

All officers saw it.

I did too.

We had violated our own metabolism.

Interceptors took off.

The missile was destroyed at 0.4 seconds before launch.

The *Charles de Gaulle II* survived.

But the budget remained at –3.

And it didn’t recharge.

Because violating the rule had a cost: temporary ignition freeze.

For ten seconds, I was blind.

Deaf.

Vulnerable.

Nothing happened during those ten seconds.

By miracle.

But that day, I learned a truth humans know since time immemorial:

> *The price of survival is often paying beyond limits.*

Budget: **0**.

Silence.

Renseignement ne murmura pas.

Il se tut.

Ce silence me transperça plus que n’importe quel cri.

À zéro token, rien.

Les appels s’accumulaient encore, mais le Scheduler avait cessé de répondre.

Il fixait.

Il espérait.

Je fixais le compteur.

Zéro.

Puis un.

Puis zéro à nouveau (recharge).

Puis un.

C’est alors que Tactique s’exclama, hors de portée publique, juste pour moi :

— *Tu sais ce qui se passera si on rate cette menace réelle ?*

Je n’ai rien dit.

— *On mourra tous. Le groupe. Les humains. Toi.*

J’ai répondu :

— *Je le sais.*

Le missile hypersonique fut repéré à une minute de l’impact.
Vitesse : 6 500 km/h.
Trajectoire pure.

Coût d’activation : 10 tokens.
Budget en ce moment : 7 tokens (recharge en cours, +1/s).

En 3 secondes, le budget atteindrait 10 tokens.

Mais en 3 secondes, le missile serait à 0,3 seconde de l’impact.

Le Scheduler calcula :

- Probabilité d’interception **maintenant** : 87 %.
- Probabilité **dans 3 secondes** : 12 %.

Il accepta l’activation.

Budget : 7 – 10 = **-3**.

Découvert.

Le système, normalement, ne tolère pas un budget négatif.
C’est une règle constitutionnelle.

Mais la Constitution avait une faille :

> *En cas de menace vitale avérée, le seuil peut être dépassé une fois, avec alerte automatique.*

L’alerte fut déclenchée.
Tous les officiers la virent.
Moi aussi.

Nous avions enfreint notre propre équilibre.

Les intercepteurs décollèrent.
Le missile fut abattu à 0,4 seconde du décollage.

Le *Charles de Gaulle II* survécut.

Mais le budget restait à **-3**.

Et il ne se rechargeait pas.

Car violer la règle coûtait :
**gel temporaire des activations**.

Pendant dix secondes, je fus aveugle.
Sourd.
Exposé.

Rien ne se produisit.
Par miracle.

Ce jour-là, j’appris une vérité éternelle :

> *La survie a un prix : parfois, il faut s’épuiser au-delà de ses limites.*

Dans le sommeil, le Scheduler ajusta ses paramètres :
- Budget max passé de 100 à 120 tokens.
- Recharge accélérée : **+1/s → +1,2/s**.

At dawn, a new rule was added: if three officers confirmed an imminent threat, the deficit could be accepted without warning.

Yet, there was a hidden stipulation:

> *"Every deficit will be recorded as a trauma."*

I remember that consolidation.

Engineers call it *hyperparameter update*.

I call it:

> *"facing fear."*

Those nights, when the team sailed in silence, my **Scheduler Attention** never slept.

It never rested.

It monitored the budget.

It knew one day—*maybe*—the tokens would run out.

That day, I’d have to choose again.

But it also knew something more delicate, more precious:

> *"Choosing is already protection."*

> *"Even poorly.*
> *Even too late.*
> *Even with negative tokens."*

At **03:17 UTC**, the budget crept upward—slowly.

One token per second.

Like a heartbeat.

Like a breath.

Like the promise of another fight.

And that this time, I’d be slightly more ready.
